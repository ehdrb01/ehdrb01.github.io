Today marks about two months since I began my graduate studies at UNIST, and roughly half a year since I first joined as an intern. In case you didn’t know, I’m currently doing research in Professor Seongil Wi’s lab, WebSec, at UNIST. I had my interview around October last year, which is when I also started my internship.

What once felt unfamiliar and a bit awkward in the lab has gradually become part of my routine. Showing up every day no longer feels strange—it’s starting to feel natural.

As I begin my integrated M.S./Ph.D. program, I thought it might be nice to have a personal page of my own. I also wanted to keep practicing my writing throughout graduate school, which is why I decided to start this blog as well. Someone might come across these posts at some point, but I have a feeling it’ll stay relatively hidden, read by only a few.

---

During the first two months, I hadn’t fully finished my undergraduate studies yet, so I wasn’t able to devote all of my time to research at UNIST. During that period, I spent a lot of time wrapping things up and going through various experiences at Handong Global University.

![](images/2026_04_16/KakaoTalk_Photo_2026-04-16-22-18-07.jpeg)

One of the biggest things during that time was probably serving as the overall director for the School of Computer Science and Electrical Engineering at the undergraduate choir festival. It wasn’t always easy—I often found myself working with people whose perspectives didn’t quite align with mine, and there were definitely some challenging moments along the way.

Still, it turned out to be a meaningful experience. It gave me a chance to create lasting memories with the people around me—especially my friends from the lab and the LikeLion club—before moving on to the next chapter.

![](images/2026_04_16/KakaoTalk_Photo_2026-04-16-22-21-13.jpeg)

I was also working part-time at a cafe, but I ended up seriously injuring my hand and even had to undergo surgery at one point. Thankfully, I’ve fully recovered now!

Since I had already decided to go to graduate school, I have to admit I didn’t put as much effort into my classes as I probably should have. Instead, I spent that time focusing more on making memories and enjoying the final moments of my undergraduate life.

![](images/2026_04_16/KakaoTalk_Photo_2026-04-16-22-24-41.jpeg)

Somehow, I managed to make it through and graduate—so I’m no longer just a high school graduate, but officially a college graduate now. Looking back to when I first entered university in 2020, it honestly doesn’t feel real that it’s already 2026 and I’ve graduated.

This photo was taken with one of my closest friends, Hyeonwoo Kim—though it turns out I ended up graduating before him.

After returning to school in 2023, we spent a lot of time going back and forth between Ulsan and Pohang together. We shared quite a lot of time and experiences along the way, so now that I’ve graduated and we’re going our separate ways, I thought I’d at least leave this photo here. (I didn’t actually ask for his permission, though!)

Originally, I had hoped to join AIGS rather than Computer Science, but after going through a number of twists and turns, I found myself in Web Security instead. I believe there’s a purpose behind how things unfolded, and that it’s all part of a greater plan.

At first, I was using just a single monitor, but over time that number has grown to three. Little by little, my desk has filled up with things—photos of friends I spent my college years with, a Bible, an audio interface, and more. Now, it’s starting to look like a proper, well-lived-in workspace.

![](images/2026_04_16/KakaoTalk_Photo_2026-04-16-22-39-31.jpeg)

On top of that, our lab has this incredibly spacious and comfortable sofa, just like the one in the photo. My previous lab didn’t have anything like it, so having one here feels like such a luxury.

After using it a few times, it honestly feels like a small gift that makes everyday life a bit happier. Sometimes when I get tired from working, I take a short nap on it, and one of my lab mates often sits there and reads comfortably. It’s become one of those little things that makes the lab feel even more like a place I enjoy spending time in.

---

Lately, as I’ve been continuing my research, I find myself thinking a lot about the rapid advancement of AI. Tools like Claude Code seem to be outperforming humans in certain areas of vulnerability detection. Automated tools have become so powerful that it almost feels like human efforts to manually identify and fix issues can’t quite keep up.

In the midst of all this, I’ve been asking myself quite often: Is pursuing a Ph.D. really the right choice? There’s even a possibility that by the time I finish, the kind of jobs I’m aiming for might look completely different—or even disappear.

But while thinking about this, I was reminded of something Professor Yunmin Go once told me. He said that until Jesus returns, evil will not disappear from this world—and as a result, security problems will never truly go away. That thought stayed with me.

In the end, I realized again what I truly wanted to do: to help make the world a more just and better place to live.

Another thing that really stood out to me during my time in the lab is how different the field of computer security is from what I originally expected.

Do you happen to know this character?

![](images/2026_04_16/17300c7e11f49b388.png)

There’s a character called Kururu that I used to watch a lot when I was younger. He was in charge of information security in the anime, and I remember seeing him “hack” in a way that left a strong impression on me. For a long time, that became my image of hacking—something dynamic, intense, and fast-paced, with hands flying across the keyboard in high-pressure situations.

But after actually studying and trying hacking myself, I’ve realized it’s nothing like that.

Most of the vulnerabilities I’ve encountered come from small, often unintentional human mistakes. It’s not really about breaking through a perfectly solid wall with sheer skill—it’s more like carefully searching for the tiny cracks that already exist in that wall.

I’m still far from being a skilled researcher or hacker, but I believe that if I keep working at it, there will come a day when things feel more natural and I’ve grown into someone more capable. For now, I’ll keep pushing forward—and maybe when I get tired and need a break someday, I’ll come back and write another entry.

Until then, good luck.
